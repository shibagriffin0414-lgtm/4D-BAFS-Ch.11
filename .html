Chapter Digest

1. Introduction to Depreciation

Non-current assets provide long-term benefits (>1 accounting period). Under accrual accounting, the cost is not fully expensed in the acquisition year. Instead, it is allocated via depreciation over its useful life. Applies only to tangible assets.

2. Capital vs. Revenue Expenditure

Capital Expenditure provides long-term benefits and is capitalized (recorded as an asset). It includes the purchase price and directly attributable costs (freight, installation, testing). Revenue Expenditure provides short-term benefits (day-to-day ops) like rent, wages, and regular repairs. Exception: Repairs required before an asset is ready for use are capitalized.

3. Commonly Used Depreciation Methods

1. Straight-Line: Constant charge each period. Formula: (Cost - Residual Value) / Useful Life.

2. Reducing-Balance: Diminishing charge. Formula: Net Book Value (NBV) × Fixed % Rate.

3. Usage-Based: Varies with output. Formula: [(Cost - Residual) / Total Estimated Output] × Actual Output.

4. Accounting Entries

To record depreciation: Dr Depreciation (Expense) | Cr Accumulated Depreciation (Contra-asset).
To transfer at year-end: Dr Profit & Loss | Cr Depreciation.

5. Disposal of Non-Current Assets

Steps to dispose:

1. Transfer cost: Dr Disposal | Cr Non-current Asset

2. Transfer accumulated dep: Dr Accumulated Dep | Cr Disposal

3. Record proceeds: Dr Cash/Bank | Cr Disposal

4. Balance is Gain (Cr Disposal) or Loss (Dr Disposal).
